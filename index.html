Site Percolation

Percolation theory has its origins in the study of porous media: What's the critical density of a medium such that a liquid can pass through it? It has since found application in epidemiology (how dense does a population have to be for a disease to spread?) or ecology (how many trees per area until a forest fire can spread through the whole system?).

All these questions can be formulated as "how many entities per area/volume do we need to form a giant component - a component which includes a non-vanishing part of the whole system?". In this explorable, we demonstrate that there's a critical density of occupied lattice site which separates two phases: one where no giant component exists (no liquid can pass through) and one where there exists a giant component (liquid may pass).



Developed for SFI's Complexity Explorer by Ben F. Maier in collaboration with the Robert Koch-Institute.

The Model

On a square lattice of side length N, each lattice site is occupied with probability p. when two occupied sites touch, they connect to build a component. If a third site touches one of the first, it belongs to the same component and so forth. With increasing occupation probability p clusters can potentially grow.
